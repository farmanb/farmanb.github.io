    Section 2 Comparison to McNeese

    In a meeting on November 14, 2022, Anne commented that we were already significantly behind McNeese in terms of majors and degrees awarded. The following is a comparison with McNeese drafted after the meeting.

    Subsection 2.1 Marketing and Recruiting

    McNeese markets its program to students in two ways. On the one hand, it boasts a track record of placing students in Ph.D. programs. Of their listed placements, AMS Group II institutions appear the most frequently (Auburn, LSU, and Texas A & M) with a couple in the lower ranked AMS Group III institutions (ULL and Baylor) and one AMS Group I Public (Oregon state). If I were a prospective student interested in pursuing mathematics, this would suggest to me that McNeese is capable of placing its graduates in predominantly good Ph.D. programs, with some exceptional students landing in top tier graduate programs.
    On the other hand, they also indicate that their students will be well-equipped to pursue careers in education or BIG (Business, Industry, and Government). This claim is backed up by their strong curriculum (see below) and the ‘extracurriculars’ offered by the department:
    • The math department appears to have several scholarships they can offer to students.
      • This seems related to Faisal’s comment that we have been losing promising students in recent years due to changes in funding sources for math majors.
      • It’s not clear to me that we have any control over such things at the moment.
    • The math department claims to maintain an active MAA chapter.
      • This provides students with a connection to the regional mathematics community through events like the LA/MS sectional, where students could participate in events such as poster sessions, paper competitions, and an integration bee.
      • The MAA also provides a great deal of resources to enhance undergraduate participation in mathematics. For example, the Undergraduate Research Special Interest Group of the MAA (SIGMAA UR) provides resources to support faculty in engaging undergraduates in research projects, which can be a significant asset when they apply to jobs or graduate schools.
      • The MAA is laser focused on undergraduate education. They provide professional development programs and funding opportunities that are primarily aimed at undergraduates and underserved populations.
    • Professional networking through Pi Mu Epsilon, the national mathematics honor society.
      • This provides students with a connection to the national mathematics community through MathFest, which is an annual meeting of mathematicians hosted by the MAA and PME. I attended in Philly this year, and noticed a lot of undergraduates in talks, poster sessions, events, etc.
    We do list possible careers for students who major in mathematics, but it doesn’t look like we have evidence that our students are actually being placed in any of these positions. Perhaps we could use what data we have on our graduates to update the math program’s website to reflect outcomes for our students? I feel like we could make a more compelling case to convince students to major in math if we could provide them data such as
    • How likely is it they’ll leave the program having passed actuarial exams?
    • How many of our actuarial students are placed in jobs where they’re using the skills they acquired at ULM?
    • Where have we historically placed our students who want to continue on to graduate study?
    • Do we have any data on students who have landed jobs at well-known BIG employers, either regionally or nationally?
    Besides placement data, it might also be beneficial to tap into our alumni network to improve student outcomes and thereby improve recruitment. Do we maintain sufficiently strong connections to our graduates so that we could potentially pull some of them back in for networking events? For example, could we invite some successful students back to give a talk about their experience? It might be beneficial to have them share their experience with finding their current job and to provide advice for current students seeking a similar path.
    • If we could promote a culture of networking/connection, that could significantly improve outcomes for our students interested in careers in BIG.
    • Events like this could also foster a sense of community for our majors, and maybe even help us build the critical mass we need to get a math club off the ground, which would lay the groundwork for a PME chapter.
    • Moreover, if we assume the vast majority of our students are indeed either local or part of a specific community (e.g. the Nepalese community), then this could help create a word-of-mouth recruiting network. Students who have a positive experience might suggest ULM to others they know are looking for similar experiences/outcomes.

    Subsection 2.2 Academic Program

    McNeese seems to offer several concentrations: Computational Science, Mathematical Physics, Mathematics, Statistics, Math Education, and Physics Education. Aside from the education concentrations, each one seems to be modeled roughly around the same core set of math courses formed by the Mathematics concentration with some modifications to elective choices. The primary advantage that McNeese seems to have is their 8 tenured/tenure-track faculty, which I would assume makes it easier for them to offer the necessary elective courses more frequently.
    Besides the curricular uniformity across concentrations, the most notable difference I can see between the two programs is the level of rigor. While McNeese and ULM both require about the same number of math or math-related electives at about the same level, McNeese requires its students to take significantly more traditional advanced mathematics courses than we do.
    Our two B.S. programs agree in a total of eight math courses that one would expect from any math degree in the country:
    • Calculus I, II, and III (our 1031, 1032, and 2032);
    • Linear algebra (our 2002);
    • Foundations of Math (our 2040);
    • Probability and Statistics (our 3003);
    • Analysis/Advanced Calculus (our 3080); and
    • Modern Algebra (our 3086).
    Beyond these eight courses, ULM requires students to take 3 free math electives and Math 3040, which does not introduce any material not already covered in 3080 and 3086.
    By contrast, McNeese requires approximately the same number of free math electives and five additional courses that include material not covered in the courses listed above:
    • A semester long course on mathematical software.
      • The description is vague, but it appears this course is devoted to learning how to use a package along the lines of Sage, Mathematica, Matlab, Maple, etc.
      • Having taught Calculus III this semester, I think our students would benefit immensely from a course like this.
    • One semester of (Ordinary) Differential Equations (equivalent to Math 3001).
      • This seems standard fare for students who might go on to do applied math, either at the graduate level or in BIG careers.
    • Two semesters of Numerical Methods
      • The first semester covers methods from calculus (e.g. numerical integration/differentiation, etc.) and linear algebra (e.g. Gaussian elimination, matrix inversion, LU decomposition, etc.).
      • The second semester seems to be focused on numerical solutions to ODEs and PDEs. This also seems important for students who want to pursue either applied math or a BIG career.
    • One semester of Seminar
      • This course requires students to read papers and present, which would help develop skills valuable to students aiming for either academic or BIG careers.
    • 1One of the following (not required for the other concentrations)
    • A second semester of Probability and Statistics
      • I assume this is roughly equivalent to Math 4003.
    • A second semester of Advanced Calculus.
      • The course description seems to suggest this is roughly equivalent to our Math 4017.
    • A second semester of Modern Algebra
      • The description is very vague. It might be equivalent to our Math 4025. It might also be equivalent to 3086 being split into a year long sequence.
    A cursory glance would seem to imply that McNeese is recruiting from a more talented pool than we can access. However, it is important to note that McNeese’s degree plan assumes that students start their Freshman year taking PreCalculus with a College Algebra corequisite. Compared to ULM’s assumption that math majors start their Freshman year in Calculus, it would appear that McNeese is doing more with their majors in four years than we are.
    Under the assumption that McNeese and ULM are indeed relatively similar institutions, I would surmise that, rather than adding new concentrations, we might benefit the most from emulating their recruiting methods to draw in new students and streamlining our curriculum design to keep students on track to graduate in four years.
    It is my assumption that by forcing all students — regardless of concentration — to take essentially the same core math classes they are able to keep enrollment in those classes at a relatively steady level from year to year, allowing students to take the courses they need to graduate at the right time.
    Additionally, if we’re observing in our student population what we perceive to be a decline in aptitude, perhaps we should re-evaluate the engagement and support we provide our majors. Unfortunately, I really do not have much insight into this problem. Maybe it would be valuable to meet at some point to identify the root cause and brainstorm how we might address it?

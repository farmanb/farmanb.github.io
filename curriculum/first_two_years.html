    Section 2.2 The First Two Years

    “The most important task of the first two years is to move students from a procedural/computational understanding of mathematics to a broad understanding encompassing logical reasoning, generalization, abstraction, and formal proof. This should be achieved as soon as possible in a student’s undergraduate career.” [4]

    Subsection 2.2.1 Content

    • “There should be a balance between the development of computational skill, conceptual understanding, theoretical reasoning, and applications.” [4]
    • “There should be an attempt to phase in logical language starting in the freshman year, rather than a sudden jump in the sophomore or junior year.” [4]
    • “Students should have the opportunity to sample a wider variety of topics. Such topics should include mathematics that is of recent origin or current interest, illustrating our discipline as a continually growing and developing body of knowledge, driven by creativity, a passionate search for fundamental structure and interrelationships, and a methodology that is both powerful and intellectually compelling. Students should encounter mathematics in an interesting historical, aesthetic or useful context. Prospective high school teachers need a deepening of their understanding of high school mathematics. Alternatives to calculus and linear algebra should be available in the first two years. Some possibilities are: discrete mathematics, number theory, geometry, and knot theory.” [4]
    On paper, our students take four to seven mathematics courses in the first two years:
    • Math 1016 Elementary Statistics (Actuarial Science)
    • Math 1031 Calculus I
    • Math 1032 Calculus II
    • Math 2032 Calculus III
    • Math 2002 Applied Linear Algebra
    • Math 2040 Foundations of Mathematics (Mathematics and Actuarial Sciences)
    • Math 3003 Mathematical Statistics (Actuarial Sciences)
    Math 2040 is the course in which logic and the techniques of proof are introduced. Unfortunately, data from current or recently graduated majors suggests that, in practice, most of our majors are not taking Math 2040 in their first two years.
    Consequently, the only experience many majors have in the first two years are in courses that focus on computation. These students later find themselves facing a very steep learning curve in their final two years when they reach courses that introduce and/or reinforce proof writing skills.
    • “I do wish I would’ve started proof writing before 2040. Or start 2040 sooner. I had never written a proof before and it took me a while to get the hang of it.” — Math Major Exit Survey, Fall 2023.
    • “Low level proof writing classes would also likely be beneficial, 2040 does a pretty good job as an introduction to proofs though more would be ideal.” — Math Major Exit Survey, Fall 2023.
    Moreover, our elective offerings are very sparse:
    • Math 3001 Differential Equations
      • Prerequisite: Math 1032 Calculus II
    • Math 3037 Number Theory
      • Prerequisite: Math 2040 Foundations of Mathematics
    • Math 4009 Cryptography
      • Prerequisite: Math 2040 Foundations of Mathematics
    While it should be possible for students to satisfy the prerequisites within the first two years, this does not seem to happen in practice. Anecdotally, various course sequencing or other timing issues seem to force students into taking the upper level actuarial science offerings to fill elective requirements, even when they do not have interest in these courses.
    “I would have liked more variation in the course offerings towards the end of my schooling. I really enjoyed classes like cryptography and number theory and found calculus based classes very interesting, but I have little to no interest in actuarial math classes. Classes I would have liked to see would do more with theoretical math as opposed to applied math, though I understand that may be harder to generate interest for. Low level proof writing classes would also likely be beneficial, 2040 does a pretty good job as an introduction to proofs though more would be ideal.” — Math Major Exit Survey, Fall 2023.

    Subsection 2.2.2 Technology

    “Prospective mathematics majors should be familiar with appropriate use of technology. They should, in particular, have experience with a computer algebra system during the first two years of undergraduate training.” [4]
    Currently, ULM does not officially provide any training for Computer Algebra Systems (CAS) or other technological aspects of mathematics, e.g. \(\LaTeX\) usage training for students. While there may be some training built into various courses, adoption seems sporadic at best.
    “I would like to see more use of math and science centric programs like LaTeX and more focus on what math is like after school.” — Math Major Exit Survey, Fall 2023.
    We should consider repurposing Math 3073 as a course that runs alongside Math 1031 that teaches students to use some sort of CAS, e.g.
    • SageMath (Free)
    • Wolfram Mathematica ($815 per license, per year)
    • Maple (Must request quote)
    • MathWorks Matlab (Must request quote)

    Subsection 2.2.3 Recruitment

    • “If you want to recruit, teach good courses. We lose a lot of students who come in thinking that they want to be math majors by failing to make real to them the intellectual vitality of mathematics. It is important to assign excellent teachers to the pipeline courses.” [4]
    • “The profession will pay a dear price if it only encourages the elite to pursue majors in mathematics.” [4]
    We are addressing some of the pipeline issues by inserting tenure stream faculty into the calculus sequence for recruiting purposes. However, anecdotally, there seems to be a prevailing attitude that we do not have majors because we do not have access to a pool of students who are “smart enough” to do math. Arguably, we are paying the “dear price” as a result — according to CAES data, our majors are down significantly.
    Fortunately, the report from CRAFTY [4] provides some advice to bolster recruitment efforts.
    • Put topics and activities into service courses that will attract students to mathematics.
    • Present recent developments in mathematics. Students will be excited by material that is interesting, engaging, and intellectually challenging but accessible.
    • Use simulations, visualizations and approximations in the presentation of mathematics. Mathematical modeling, which can dramatically illustrate the applicability of mathematics, can draw students into the major.
    • Enrich service courses with applications that will both serve students in other departments well and show students the value of mathematics. This possibility is enhanced in calculus courses directed at specific scientific disciplines.
    • Promote the idea of mathematics as a profession, with evidence that a major in mathematics is a marketable degree, assistance in obtaining internships, and information about career opportunities in mathematics.
    • Seek out students in calculus courses, invite them to be mathematics majors and help them with any necessary paperwork (e.g., by filling out the declaration of major form for them).
    • Integrate students into the social as well as the academic life of the department (e.g., by having a departmental lounge accessible to students).
    • Employ students in various ways. Jobs, from instructional and tutorial functions to simple clerical duties, can be a powerful draw into the mathematics major. It can greatly add to the students’ feelings of involvement in a mathematics community.
    • Promote the formation of math clubs, MAA student chapters, Pi Mu Epsilon chapters, Math Circles or other organized mathematics activities.
    • Encourage double majors.

    Section 2.3 Upper Division

    “Mathematics grows through the construction of abstract theories from definitions, examples, and theorems. Students learn to cope with such complexity by grappling with clusters of related ideas, in depth and over an extended period. Every mathematics major student should encounter at least one area in depth, drawing on ideas and tools from previous courses and making connections among them.” [2]

    Subsection 2.3.1 Proof

    As mathematics is constructed from axioms using deductive reasoning, proof is, without question, an essential component of mathematics. These techniques are introduced in Math 2040, but require reinforcement through upper division courses. Real Analysis (Math 3080, Math 4017) and Abstract Algebra (Math 3086, Math 4025) are the natural outlets because they provide the basic techniques for virtually every subfield of mathematics, save discrete mathematics.
    Our inability to regularly offer analysis and algebra courses is adversely affecting our students. Recently, in order to graduate students, inappropriate substitutions have been made for these courses:
    • History of Mathematics (Math 4007) has been used to replace Advanced Real Analysis (Math 4017).
    • Differential Equations (Math 3001) has been used to replace Real Analysis (Math 3080).
    • Number Theory (Math 3037) has been used to replace Modern Algebra (Math 3086).
    Consequently, we are graduating students without adequately developing the skills one expects from the holder of a B.S. in Mathematics. This makes our students less competitive in graduate school and job applications, and weakens the perceived strength of the program.

    Subsection 2.3.2 Depth

    In its current form, the mathematics major lacks coherence. For a mathematics major, we require (on paper) that students take three courses that utilize the tools developed in Math 2040:
    • Real Analysis (Math 3080)
    • Modern Algebra (Math 3086)
    • Introduction to Analysis and Algebra (Math 3040)
      • This course does not cover any material that is not already covered in Real Analysis and Modern Algebra.
    The remaining mathematics requirements are three credits at the 3000 level and six credits at the 4000 level. There are several courses that build on the concepts from these courses on the books
    • Math 4002 Linear Spaces
    • Math 4006 Theory of Functions of a Complex Variable
    • Math 4017 Advanced Real Analysis
    • Math 4019 Topology
    • Math 4025 Advanced Modern Algebra
    However, none of these courses has run in the past four years.

    Subsection 2.3.3 Beyond the Classroom

    Subsubsection 2.3.3.1 Seminars, Capstones, etc.

    “Every major student should have a “high impact” experience that requires substantial work in mathematics outside the carefully scripted confines of ordinary course work. Students should present their results in written and oral forms. Institutions can provide this opportunity in various ways: undergraduate research experiences, courses driven by inquiry or open-ended problem solving, capstone courses, internships or jobs with a substantial mathematical component, etc.” [2]

    Subsubsection 2.3.3.2 Career Preparation

    “Departments can employ both “static” information—posters, links on department websites, career brochures, career information from professional societies—and more dynamic strategies: inviting external speakers (including alumni); short, self-contained, career-focused orientations or assignments within courses; and encouraging and supporting students’ attendance at regional and national professional meetings.” [2]
    Generally, the program lacks an overview of career paths for its graduates. Currently, anecdotal evidence suggests that students see only two paths: teacher or actuary. This year, students were able to attend a career information session at the LA/MS sectional MAA meeting this year. This experience helped students to see that there are many more opportunities:
    “Attending the workshops and participating in the competition at MAA was a truly enriching experience for me. I gained valuable insights into the prospects of a math major and discovered new career paths within mathematics that I found intriguing. The opportunity to learn from experts in the field and engage with fellow enthusiasts was both inspiring and educational.”
    However, since we cannot take all of the students, we should consider ways to provide more career advice to our students as above.
